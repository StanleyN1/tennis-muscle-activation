Research Sources & Key Findings

[1] Chow JW, Carlton LG, Lim Y-T, Shim J-H, Chae W-S, Kuenster AF (1999)
Muscle activation during the tennis volley. Medicine & Science in Sports & Exercise, 31(6):846–854.
PMID 10378912

Key Findings (Primary Source)

"The extensor carpi radialis was more active than the flexor carpi radialis during both forehand and backhand volleys, suggesting the importance of wrist extension/abduction and grip strength."
Subjects "did not tighten their grip and wrist until moments before ball impact" — FCR/ECR peaked in the forward swing phase immediately before contact.
Triceps brachii activity was "clearly greater" in backhand than forehand trials; attributed to "greater elbow extension during the backhand volley."
PCM was significantly more active in forehand forward swing than backhand (P=0.037), indicating a "greater role for the pectoralis major than previously believed."
In the ready phase, erector spinae was most active among monitored muscles: players "leaned forward slightly and moved the line of gravity toward the balls of the feet" to prepare for forward movement.
External oblique co-contractions during active phases: ipsilateral OB functioned as "ipsilateral lateral flexor" while contralateral OB served as "contralateral axial rotator."
[2] Chow JW, Tillman MD, Knudson DV, Andrew DPS (2007)
Pre- and post-impact muscle activation in the tennis volley. British Journal of Sports Medicine, 41(11):754–759.
doi:10.1136/bjsm.2007.037184

Key Findings · Pre vs. Post-Impact EMG (%MVC)

MusclePre-ImpactPost-Impact

Anterior deltoid dominates pre-impact for racket positioning; ECR dominates post-impact for wrist stabilization and shock absorption.
Ball speed effects: pre-impact activation increased ~12% from slow to fast balls; post-impact increased ~10%, showing players anticipate impact demands.
"Concerns over possible arm injuries due to larger tennis ball are unjustified" — ball size showed no meaningful differences in muscle activation patterns.

doi:10.1136/bjsm.2007.037333

Scapular stabilizers fire in strict proximal-to-distal sequence: serratus anterior and trapezius activate before the arm positioners and rotator cuff muscles.
Infraspinatus activates only after ball impact (+47 ms), suggesting a fine-tuning and deceleration role rather than a propulsive one.
[4] Ryu RKN, McCormick J, Jobe FW, Moynes DR, Antonelli DJ (1988)
An electromyographic analysis of shoulder function in tennis players. American Journal of Sports Medicine, 16(5):481–485.
doi:10.1177/036354658801600509

Key Findings (Foundational Shoulder EMG Study)

Serve & forehand: "Subscapularis, pectoralis major, and serratus anterior display the greatest activity" — establishing these as the primary drivers of internal rotation and shoulder acceleration.
Backhand acceleration & follow-through: "Middle deltoid, supraspinatus, and infraspinatus are most active" — confirming the posterior shoulder dominance in the backhand.
Biceps brachii demonstrated increased activity during cocking and follow-through phases across all strokes, providing elbow flexion stability and glenohumeral compression.
Tennis strokes involve "complex sequences of muscle activity that incorporate contributions from the lower extremities and trunk into smooth, coordinated patterns" — an early articulation of the kinetic chain concept in tennis.
[5] Chow JW, Shim JH, Lim YT (2003)
Lower trunk muscle activity during the tennis serve. Journal of Science and Medicine in Sport, 6(4):512–518.
PMID 14723400 · doi:10.1016/s1440-2440(03)80276-1

Key Findings · Trunk Muscles During the Serve

"No major differences in muscle activation pattern were found across different serve types" (flat, topspin, slice) — trunk activation is largely consistent regardless of serve style.
"Co-activation of lower trunk muscles may help to stabilise the lumbar spine during the arch back and forward swing phases" — bilateral erector spinae and oblique co-contractions form a protective corset.
"Bilateral asymmetries were more pronounced in rectus abdominis and external oblique than in internal oblique and erector spinae muscles" — the superficial abdominal wall shows stronger lateralization than deep muscles.
Abdominal muscles showed greater activity during upward racket swing in topspin serves compared to flat serves — subtle differences exist even where overall patterns are similar.
[6] Rogowski I, Rouffet D, Lambalot F, Brosseau O, Hautier C (2011)
Trunk and upper limb muscle activation during flat and topspin forehand drives in young tennis players. Journal of Applied Biomechanics, 27(1):15–21.
doi:10.1123/jab.27.1.15

Key Findings · Flat vs. Topspin Forehand

"Shoulder muscle activity rose from flat to topspin forehand drives in less-skilled players, but not for higher-skilled players" — elite players recruit shoulder muscles more economically regardless of spin type.
"Forearm muscle activity reduced from flat to topspin forehand drives in skilled players" but was similar in less-skilled players — expert efficiency in wrist/forearm control.
"Radial deviation proved more effective than shoulder abduction for increasing vertical racket velocity" when transitioning from flat to topspin — wrist mechanics are a key performance differentiator.
Practical implication: higher overall EMG does not equal better performance; expert players achieve greater ball velocity with more selective, economical muscle recruitment.
[7] Tai M-L, Yang C-J, Tang W-T, Elliott B, Chang K-L (2022)
Upper extremity muscle activation during drive volley and groundstroke for two-handed backhand of female tennis players. Journal of Sports Science & Medicine, 21(4):586–594.
doi:10.52082/jssm.2022.586

Key Findings · Drive Volley vs. Groundstroke (2H Backhand)

"Drive volleys showed greater extensor carpi radialis and triceps brachii activation in the trailing arm, enabling a shortened backswing in the preparation phase" — the arm must compensate for reduced preparation time.
Leading (non-dominant) arm FCR was significantly higher in drive volleys (52.50 ± 20.52%) versus groundstrokes (40.39 ± 13.91%) — the non-dominant arm contributes more to urgent volley situations.
"The drive volley and groundstroke require a relatively firm grip near impact" due to high external forces — both strokes require protective co-contraction of wrist flexors and extensors at contact.
[8] Dong R, Su X, Li S, Ni X, Liu Y (2024)
Characteristics, relationships, and differences in muscle activity and impact load attenuation during tennis forehand stroke with different grips. Life (Basel), 14(11):1433.
doi:10.3390/life14111433

Key Findings · Eastern vs. Western Grip

Flexor carpi ulnaris RMS was significantly higher in Eastern grip (0.97 ± 0.21) compared to Western grip (0.76 ± 0.30, p=0.037) — Eastern grip demands more active wrist flexion stabilization.
Extensor digitorum communis contributed more to impact load attenuation in the Western grip — finger extensors play a greater shock-absorbing role when the wrist is more pronated.
Forearm muscles showed "excellent coordination across both grip styles, with cross-correlation coefficients exceeding 0.90, indicating a high degree of synchronized contraction" — co-activation pattern is robust across technique.
Eastern grip produced higher peak wrist acceleration on the ulnar side (p=0.016) but exhibited more rapid load attenuation — grip choice trades off force application speed vs. attenuation capacity.
